### Q: Measure calibration using reliability diagrams, ECE variants, NLL, Brier score, and selective risk.
* **Difficulty:** Senior
* **Category:** Calibration
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** Calibration asks whether events predicted at probability $p$ occur about $p$ of the time. Use reliability plots and proper scoring rules, treat ECE as a binning-dependent summary, and evaluate risk versus coverage when abstention is allowed.
* **The Deep Dive:** Partition predictions into bins and plot mean confidence against empirical outcome frequency with support bars. Standard ECE is $\sum_b(n_b/N)|\operatorname{acc}(b)-\operatorname{conf}(b)|$; equal-width, equal-mass, classwise, adaptive, and debiased variants answer different questions. ECE can be manipulated through bin choices and hides cancellation within bins. NLL and Brier are strictly proper and assess sharpness plus calibration; Brier decomposes into reliability, resolution, and uncertainty. For multiclass models, inspect top-label and classwise calibration. If the system may abstain, plot selective risk—error among accepted examples—against coverage. Check calibration by slice, time, and decision region; fit temperature, isotonic, Platt, or vector scaling on held-out data only.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** A calibrated global score may be miscalibrated for rare groups or under shift. Isotonic methods overfit small sets; temperature scaling preserves ranking but may not fix class-specific error. Confidence is not epistemic certainty.
* **Red Flag:** Reporting one ECE number without bins, supports, proper scoring rules, or a reliability plot.
