### Q: Design visual document retrieval: ColPali-style late interaction over page images versus OCR-then-text pipelines.
* **Difficulty:** Principal
* **Category:** Retrieval
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** ColPali-style systems embed each page image as roughly a thousand patch vectors and score queries with ColBERT-style MaxSim, skipping OCR entirely and preserving layout, tables, and figures; the price is a multi-vector index orders of magnitude larger than single-vector text, so production designs use a coarse first stage plus MaxSim rescoring or route between visual and text pipelines per page.
* **The Deep Dive:** The ColPali recipe runs a vision-language backbone (originally PaliGemma-3B) over a rendered page image, producing on the order of 1,000 patch-level token embeddings from a 32x32 patch grid, each projected down to 128 dimensions. A text query is embedded into per-token vectors by the same model, and relevance is late interaction: $s(q, p) = \sum_i \max_j \, q_i^\top d_j$, summing over query tokens $i$ the best-matching page patch $j$. Training is contrastive over query-page pairs, so patches containing the answer region—table cells, chart labels, a diagram callout—learn to align with query tokens. Because scoring happens over spatially grounded patches, the model retrieves pages whose evidence is purely visual, and the max operation localizes which region matched, which is useful for highlighting.

  The OCR-then-text alternative chains OCR, layout analysis, reading-order reconstruction, table structure recovery, and often figure captioning before chunking and embedding. Every stage has an error rate and they compound: a mis-detected column order scrambles a table's semantics even at perfect character accuracy, and charts contribute nothing unless a captioning model verbalizes them. On the ViDoRe benchmark built to test exactly this, ColPali outperformed strong OCR-plus-text pipelines by a wide margin on visually rich categories (infographics, figures, scanned tables) while remaining competitive on clean text pages. The honest reading is distributional: the visual model's advantage concentrates where OCR loses information, not everywhere.

  ```text
  OCR pipeline : page -> OCR -> layout -> chunks -> 1 vector/chunk  (~4 KB fp32 @1024-d)
  ColPali      : page -> ViT patches -> ~1030 vectors x 128-d       (~527 KB fp32, ~264 KB bf16)
  ```

  That is roughly a 60 to 130x storage multiplier per unit indexed, and MaxSim cannot be served by a plain ANN index because scoring needs all patch vectors per candidate. Production designs therefore go two-stage: build a single pooled vector per page (mean or attention pooling over patches) for first-stage ANN, then fetch full patch sets for the top few hundred candidates and rescore with MaxSim; further compression comes from pooling adjacent patches (a 2x2 merge cuts storage 4x with modest quality loss) and binary or int8 quantization of patch vectors. The falsifiable test: stratify your own eval by page type—clean text, table-heavy, figure-heavy—and measure nDCG@5 per stratum for the visual pipeline, the OCR pipeline, and a router that classifies pages and sends each to one pipeline. If the visual model does not win the figure and table strata by a clear margin, or loses the clean-text stratum badly, the flat "replace OCR" architecture is falsified and routing or hybrid fusion is the correct design. Also measure first-stage recall of the pooled index against exhaustive MaxSim; if it is below roughly 95 percent at your candidate depth, the two-stage design is silently capping quality.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** Ingest flips from CPU-bound OCR to GPU-bound vision encoding—budget one GPU forward pass per page and batch aggressively—while query-side MaxSim rescoring adds tens of milliseconds for a few hundred candidates. Text pipelines keep advantages the benchmark does not measure: exact phrase search, cheap incremental re-chunking, and string-level citations, whereas visual retrieval cites a page region. Most enterprise deployments land on hybrid: text indexing for born-digital text, visual indexing for scans, slides, and figure-dense PDFs, fused with RRF, with the generation step reading the page image directly through a VLM so OCR errors never reach the answer.
* **Red Flag:** Treating benchmark superiority on visually rich documents as license to delete the text pipeline, then discovering the multi-vector index costs two orders of magnitude more per page and cannot serve exact-phrase or string-citation requirements at all.
