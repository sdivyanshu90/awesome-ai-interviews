### Q: Select an embedding model: MTEB/BEIR pitfalls, leaderboard contamination, and building a domain evaluation harness.
* **Difficulty:** Mid
* **Category:** Evaluation
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** Leaderboard rank is a weak prior, not a decision: MTEB averages unrelated tasks and its public test sets leak into training data, so shortlist on the retrieval subset plus operational constraints, then decide on a labeled harness built from your own queries and corpus.
* **The Deep Dive:** The MTEB average blends retrieval, STS, classification, and clustering, so a model can climb the overall table on tasks irrelevant to search; for RAG only the retrieval and reranking scores matter, and even those are dominated by BEIR-style datasets whose corpora and test queries have been public for years. Contamination is structural: modern embedding models train on massive scraped pair data, and several well-documented leaderboard climbs came from tuning on data distributionally identical—or literally overlapping—with benchmark test sets. The result is score inflation that does not transfer: a model can lead a public benchmark by two points and lose on your ticket corpus by five. A second, subtler mismatch is task geometry. Asymmetric retrieval (short query against long passage) needs a model trained for that direction, often with instruction prefixes such as a query-versus-passage prompt; picking a symmetric STS-optimized model for asymmetric search reliably underperforms, and silently dropping the required prefix at index or query time is one of the most common integration bugs.

  A domain harness needs three ingredients. First, golden queries: sample 100 to 300 real queries from logs (or subject-matter experts pre-launch), stratified by intent—lookup, how-to, troubleshooting, multilingual—because aggregate numbers hide slice regressions. Second, labels via pooling: for each query, union the top 20 results from every candidate model plus a BM25 baseline, judge the pool with an LLM rubric, and human-verify a 10 to 20 percent sample; pooling matters because judging only one model's results biases recall in its favor. Third, metrics with uncertainty: recall@10 and nDCG@10 per slice with bootstrap confidence intervals over queries, since with 150 queries a two-point gap is often noise. Always include BM25 and your incumbent model as baselines: if BM25 wins any slice, that slice is lexical and your architecture needs hybrid, not a better embedder.

  The falsifiable claim worth testing is that the leaderboard predicts your domain. Rank five to eight candidate models by MTEB retrieval average and by your harness, then compare orderings (Kendall's $\tau$ or simply whether the leaderboard's top pick is in your harness's top two). Teams that run this routinely find inversions—the leaderboard leader beaten on their corpus by a smaller, cheaper model—and each inversion quantifies exactly the money the harness just saved. If instead the orderings agree tightly across several corpora and slices, the harness is redundant for your domain and the cheap decision procedure is defensible; that is the experiment that would falsify this whole answer, and it usually fails to.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** Score is one column in the decision matrix. Dimension drives index cost forever: at 100M chunks, a 3072-d float32 index costs four times the memory of a 768-d one for a few points of quality. Latency and throughput on your hardware bound ingest and query rates; maximum sequence length must cover your chunk size; license matters (some strong open models ship research-only or copyleft terms); multilingual coverage must match your traffic; and API-served models add per-token cost plus deprecation risk—when the provider retires the model, you re-embed everything, so pin versions and budget the migration path from day one.
* **Red Flag:** Choosing the current MTEB overall leader without checking the retrieval subset, the license, the instruction-prefix contract, or a single query from the actual domain—then blaming RAG quality problems on the LLM.
