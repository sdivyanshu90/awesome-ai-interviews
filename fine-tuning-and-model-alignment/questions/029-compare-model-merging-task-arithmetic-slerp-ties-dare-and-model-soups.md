### Q: Compare model merging: task arithmetic, SLERP, TIES and DARE, and model soups — when merging beats multi-task SFT.
* **Difficulty:** Senior
* **Category:** Architecture
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** Merging combines fine-tunes in weight space: task arithmetic adds scaled task vectors, TIES and DARE prune and sign-resolve them to cut interference, SLERP interpolates two checkpoints along the hypersphere, and soups average same-recipe checkpoints. It wins when you have checkpoints but not data, gradients, or GPU budget; it loses to multi-task SFT when tasks must interact or the endpoints do not share a linearly connected basin.
* **The Deep Dive:** Everything starts from the task vector: for a fine-tune $\theta_t$ of a shared base $\theta_0$, define $\tau_t=\theta_t-\theta_0$, and compose behaviors by

  $$
  \theta_{\mathrm{merged}}=\theta_0+\lambda\sum_{t=1}^{T}\tau_t,
  $$

  with $\lambda$ tuned on a held-out set (negating a $\tau_t$ removes a behavior, e.g., subtracting a toxicity fine-tune). This works at all only because fine-tunes from a common initialization tend to stay in a loss basin where linear interpolation does not cross high-loss barriers—mode connectivity is the load-bearing assumption, not an implementation detail. Naive addition degrades as $T$ grows because task vectors interfere: parameters where two tasks push in opposite signs partially cancel, and masses of small noisy deltas swamp the few large task-relevant ones. TIES attacks both directly with trim-elect-merge: trim each $\tau_t$ to its top-magnitude entries (keeping around 20 percent is typical), elect a sign per parameter by which sign carries more total magnitude across tasks, then average only the values agreeing with the elected sign. DARE is complementary and stochastic: drop a random fraction $p$ of each task vector's entries and rescale survivors by $1/(1-p)$, which preserves the delta in expectation, $\mathbb E\left[\tilde\tau\right]=\tau$; because fine-tuning deltas are extremely redundant, $p$ up to 0.9 or higher often costs nothing, and sparsifying before a TIES-style merge reduces collision probability between tasks—hence the popular DARE+TIES stack.

  SLERP is the two-model specialist: for each tensor pair with angle $\Omega$ between the flattened weights, interpolate along the great circle,

  $$
  \operatorname{slerp}(\theta_1,\theta_2;t)=\frac{\sin\left((1-t)\Omega\right)}{\sin\Omega}\,\theta_1+\frac{\sin\left(t\Omega\right)}{\sin\Omega}\,\theta_2,
  $$

  which preserves norm where linear interpolation of nearly orthogonal directions would shrink it toward the origin—one reason SLERP frequently edges out LERP on chat-model merges. Model soups sit at the other extreme of ambition: average checkpoints from the same recipe that differ only in hyperparameters or seeds; the greedy variant adds a checkpoint to the average only if held-out accuracy improves, and reliably buys a fraction of a point over the best single run at zero inference cost.

  Worked example and the falsifiable test. Merging a code fine-tune and a math fine-tune of the same base with plain task arithmetic at $\lambda=1$ typically lands below both parents on their own domains; DARE at $p=0.9$ plus TIES sign election recovers most of each parent's score because roughly nine of ten interfering coordinates are zeroed before election. The test that decides whether merging is even admissible: sweep $t$ from 0 to 1 between the two endpoints and evaluate—if accuracy stays high along the path, they share a basin and merging is sound; if it collapses toward chance mid-path, they are not linearly mode-connected (different inits, heavy training divergence, or permutation misalignment) and no merge recipe will save you. That is also the limiting case: models with different architectures or tokenizers have no common coordinate system, and merging is undefined, full stop. Prefer merging over multi-task SFT when the training data cannot be pooled (licensing, tenant isolation), when you need behavior toggles cheaply, or when compute for retraining does not exist; prefer multi-task SFT when tasks must compose within a single response, since weight averaging cannot create cross-task circuits that no parent contains.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** Merging is free of GPUs and gradients—minutes on CPU—which is exactly why leaderboards filled with soup-of-soups whose benchmark scores outrun their usefulness; evaluation traps include merged models that keep MMLU-style scores while breaking chat-template adherence, refusal behavior, or tool-call formatting, so gate merges on instruction-following and safety suites, not knowledge benchmarks. Merge hyperparameters ($\lambda$, trim ratio, $p$) interact and need a held-out sweep; and provenance matters operationally—record parent hashes and recipes, because a bad merge two steps upstream is otherwise undebuggable.
* **Red Flag:** Merging checkpoints that do not share a base initialization and expecting task arithmetic to work, or validating a merge purely on static knowledge benchmarks while never testing format adherence, refusals, and the interpolation-path sanity check.
