### Q: Explain LLM distillation as post-training: teacher-trace SFT, logit distillation, forward versus reverse KL, and on-policy GKD.
* **Difficulty:** Senior
* **Category:** Training
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** Distillation ranges from hard sequence-level transfer—SFT on sampled teacher outputs, the R1-distill pattern—to soft logit matching against the teacher's full token distributions. The divergence direction matters: forward KL is mode-covering and can smear the student across modes it cannot fit, reverse KL is mode-seeking; and GKD fixes the exposure-bias mismatch by scoring student-sampled prefixes with the teacher instead of training only on teacher-written text.
* **The Deep Dive:** Teacher-trace SFT is the workhorse: sample completions from the teacher, filter them (for reasoning, keep only traces whose final answers pass a verifier—rejection sampling makes the data quality-gated rather than merely fluent), then run ordinary cross-entropy fine-tuning on the student. This is exactly the R1-distill recipe—roughly 800k reasoning-trace samples from the large RL-trained model fine-tuned into Qwen and Llama students with no RL at all—and it outperformed running RL directly on the small models, because the small models could imitate reasoning patterns they were unlikely to discover by exploration. Logit distillation instead matches distributions token by token: with teacher $p$ and student $q_\theta$, minimize a divergence between $p(\cdot\mid x,y_{<t})$ and $q_\theta(\cdot\mid x,y_{<t})$ over the vocabulary, usually with temperature softening. It transmits far more bits per token (the full ranking of plausible next tokens, the "dark knowledge"), but requires white-box teacher access and an identical tokenizer, which is why cross-vendor distillation is almost always trace-level.

  The choice of divergence has a geometry. Forward KL,

  $$
  D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(p\,\|\,q_\theta\right)=\sum_{y} p(y)\log\frac{p(y)}{q_\theta(y)},
  $$

  explodes wherever the teacher puts mass and the student does not, so the student is forced to cover every teacher mode; with limited capacity it spreads probability over regions between modes and can sample text the teacher would never produce. Reverse KL,

  $$
  D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\theta\,\|\,p\right)=\sum_{y} q_\theta(y)\log\frac{q_\theta(y)}{p(y)},
  $$

  is zero-forcing: the student is punished for putting mass where the teacher has none, so it concentrates on the subset of teacher modes it can actually represent—typically better generation quality from a small student, at the price of diversity. Note that plain SFT on teacher samples is a Monte-Carlo estimate of forward KL at the sequence level, so trace SFT silently inherits the mode-covering behavior.

  Both classic variants share an off-policy defect: training conditions on teacher-written prefixes, but at inference the student conditions on its own imperfect prefix. Worked example: mid-derivation the student emits an algebra slip the teacher would never make; the resulting prefix has probability near zero under the teacher data distribution, the student was never supervised there, and errors compound token by token—classic exposure bias. Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD) closes the loop: sample sequences from the student, have the teacher provide per-token distributions on those student-visited states, and minimize reverse KL or a generalized Jensen-Shannon divergence there—supervision lands exactly where the student actually goes, and empirically on-policy GKD beats off-policy distillation on summarization, translation, and reasoning at matched compute. The falsifiable comparison an interviewer should expect: at fixed token budget, off-policy trace SFT and on-policy GKD reach similar teacher-forced perplexity, but GKD wins on free-running generation metrics; if it does not, your student-teacher gap is too small for exposure bias to matter. Practical constraints round it out: many API terms of service prohibit training competing models on outputs, some open-weight licenses impose naming and attribution duties, and teacher traces can embed benchmark solutions—so decontaminate distillation sets against your eval suite exactly as you would pretraining data.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** Trace SFT is cheap, tokenizer-agnostic, and parallelizes as offline data generation; its costs are teacher inference volume and style lock-in, since students inherit teacher verbosity and formatting tics. Logit distillation needs teacher forward passes in the training loop (or terabytes of stored top-k logits) and same-tokenizer pairs; GKD adds live student sampling plus teacher scoring per batch, the most infrastructure of the three. A common production ladder: verifier-filtered trace SFT first, then on-policy GKD as a refinement stage, then optionally a small RL pass—each stage gated on free-running evals rather than perplexity.
* **Red Flag:** Treating forward and reverse KL as interchangeable "distillation losses", or claiming teacher-forced perplexity proves distillation succeeded—the mismatch that matters lives on student-generated prefixes, which teacher-forced metrics never visit.
