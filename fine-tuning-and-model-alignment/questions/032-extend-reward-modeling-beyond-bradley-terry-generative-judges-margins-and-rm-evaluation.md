### Q: Extend reward modeling beyond Bradley–Terry: generative and LLM-judge reward models, margin objectives, and evaluating the reward model itself.
* **Difficulty:** Senior
* **Category:** Alignment
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** Bradley–Terry trains a scalar head so that $\sigma(r_w-r_l)$ matches pairwise preferences; extensions add confidence margins, absolute attribute regression, and generative reward models that reason to a verdict under an explicit rubric. Evaluate the RM itself on held-out preference accuracy, adversarial RewardBench-style probes, downstream policy quality, and — decisively — its overoptimization curve, because a reward model is only as good as the policy pressure it survives.
* **The Deep Dive:** The Bradley–Terry baseline puts a scalar head on the LM and fits $p(y_w \succ y_l \mid x)=\sigma\!\left(r_\theta(x,y_w)-r_\theta(x,y_l)\right)$ with loss $-\log\sigma(\Delta)$, where $\Delta=r_\theta(x,y_w)-r_\theta(x,y_l)$. Only differences are identified — the reward is invariant to per-prompt shifts — so scores are not comparable across prompts, and compressing "why this answer is better" into one number invites shortcut features such as length and formatting. Margin objectives inject label confidence: with an annotator rating $c$ ("significantly better" versus "slightly better"), train on $-\log\sigma\!\left(\Delta-m(c)\right)$ with a larger margin $m$ for confident labels, forcing separation proportional to certainty — the Llama-2 recipe. Regression variants fit absolute per-attribute scores (helpfulness, correctness, verbosity) with squared error, HelpSteer-style, buying calibrated, decomposable rewards at the price of noisier absolute labels.

  Generative reward models replace the scalar head with the language model's own generative channel: the judge is trained (or prompted) to write a critique and then emit a verdict, with the reward read off as the probability of the verdict token or a parsed score. This buys three things scalar heads cannot: chain-of-thought before the verdict measurably improves accuracy on reasoning-heavy comparisons; judgments can be conditioned on an explicit rubric or per-prompt principles, so one judge serves many criteria without retraining; and the rationale is auditable. Inference-time compute also scales quality — sample several judgments and majority-vote. The costs are symmetric: a judgment is a full generation rather than one forward pass; pairwise judges carry position and self-preference biases, countered by order-swapping and by never letting a model family grade only itself; and verdict parsing adds failure modes a scalar head lacks. Pointwise scoring is required for cheap best-of-$n$; pairwise is more accurate but needs tournaments to rank $n$ candidates.

  Evaluating the RM is a hierarchy of increasingly expensive, increasingly decisive tests. Held-out preference accuracy comes first, but its ceiling is inter-annotator agreement — often 70 to 80 percent on general chat data — so 75 percent may be near-perfect, not mediocre. RewardBench-style probes come second: curated subsets (chat-hard, safety, reasoning) with adversarial pairs where the worse answer is longer or prettier, measuring exploitable biases directly rather than average agreement. Third, downstream measurements: best-of-$n$ selection quality against a gold judge, then full policy training. The decisive artifact is the overoptimization curve. Writing $d=\sqrt{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi\,\|\,\pi_{\mathrm{init}})}$, the Gao–Schulman–Hilton scaling fits are

  $$
  R_{\mathrm{bon}}(d)=d\,(\alpha-\beta d),\qquad R_{\mathrm{RL}}(d)=d\,(\alpha-\beta\log d),
  $$

  for gold reward, while proxy reward rises monotonically. Worked example: with $\alpha=1$, $\beta=0.1$ under best-of-$n$, gold reward peaks at $d^\ast=\alpha/2\beta=5$ with value $\alpha^2/4\beta=2.5$; optimize past $d^\ast$ and true quality falls even as the proxy climbs — Goodhart made quantitative, with the peak moving right as RM size and data grow. The falsifiable test: hold out a gold judge (or humans), plot proxy and gold against $d$ during training, and read off your safe optimization budget where the curves diverge. The limiting case that kills naive evaluation: an RM with perfect held-out accuracy can still be arbitrarily Goodhartable, because accuracy is measured on the preference-data distribution while optimization deliberately moves the policy off it — accuracy alone never certifies an RM.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** RMs go stale as the policy distribution shifts, so on-policy pipelines re-collect preferences and retrain the RM in iterations; ensembles with pessimistic aggregation (min or mean minus a variance penalty) blunt reward hacking at multiplied inference cost. Generative judges are usually too expensive to sit inside an RL inner loop at thousands of scores per update, so a common architecture uses the generative judge to label preferences and gate releases while a distilled scalar RM serves the hot path. Monitor reward-length correlation and score drift online — a rising length slope is the earliest hacking alarm.
* **Red Flag:** Equating reward-model quality with held-out or RewardBench accuracy while never measuring the overoptimization curve, or running an unbounded-KL policy against a frozen judge — both treat the RM as ground truth precisely where Goodhart guarantees it is not.
