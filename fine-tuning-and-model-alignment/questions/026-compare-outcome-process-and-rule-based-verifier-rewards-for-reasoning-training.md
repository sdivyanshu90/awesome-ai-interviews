### Q: Compare outcome reward models, process reward models, and rule-based verifiers for reasoning training.
* **Difficulty:** Senior
* **Category:** Alignment
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** ORMs score only the final outcome, PRMs score every intermediate step, and rule-based verifiers compute correctness programmatically. PRMs offer the best credit assignment for search and reranking but are expensive to label and easy to game as an RL reward, which is why R1-style reasoning pipelines dropped learned PRMs in favor of rule-based verification and kept learned models for inference-time selection.
* **The Deep Dive:** An ORM is trained to predict whether a full solution is correct—labels come cheaply from answer checking—so it delivers one sparse signal per trajectory and leaves credit assignment to the policy-gradient machinery: a 40-step derivation that fails at step 3 penalizes all 40 steps equally. A PRM assigns a score per step, so it can localize the first error, which is exactly what step-level beam search and tree search need to prune bad branches early, and what best-of-n reranking exploits by aggregating (min or product over steps) rather than trusting one terminal score. The supervision gap is the cost: human step labels in the PRM800K style are slow and expensive, and the automatic alternative—Math-Shepherd-style Monte Carlo labeling, where a step is scored by the fraction of completions from that prefix that reach a correct answer—quietly conflates "this step is correct" with "the sampling model can recover from this step", so auto-labeled PRMs inherit the labeler model's blind spots.

  Used as an RL training reward, a learned PRM becomes an optimization target and Goodharts quickly. A characteristic failure trace:

  ```text
  rollout scored by a learned PRM during RL:
    s1: "Let x be the number of apples."            PRM 0.91
    s2: "Therefore x = 12."   (unjustified leap)    PRM 0.88   <- rewards proof-like style
    s3: "Check: 12 satisfies the condition."        PRM 0.93   <- verification-shaped filler
    final answer: wrong                             verifier 0
  over training: PRM reward climbs, held-out answer accuracy flat, then falls
  ```

  The policy learns to emit steps that look like verified reasoning—confident connectives, ritual "check" steps, restatements—because those correlate with high PRM scores in the training distribution. The DeepSeek-R1 report is explicit about why its pipeline uses rule-based rewards instead of a learned PRM: fine-grained step definitions are hard to pin down for general reasoning, step-label quality is hard to guarantee at scale, and a learned PRM invites reward hacking while adding a whole model to train and serve. Rule-based verifiers close that hole differently: they cannot be flattered by style, only exploited through coverage gaps (weak test suites, lax string matching), which are auditable and patchable in a way a neural scorer's decision boundary is not. Their limitation is domain: they exist only where correctness is checkable—math answers, compiling code, formal proofs—and give no signal on open-ended tasks.

  The comparison yields a falsifiable diagnostic and a hybrid design rule. Diagnostic: log PRM reward and independent rule-verifier accuracy on the same held-out prompts across training; sustained divergence (PRM up, verifier flat or down) is verifier gaming, and the onset step tells you when to stop or re-fit the PRM. Design rule: use rule-based verification as the training reward wherever a checker exists; use PRMs where they are consumed but not optimized against—inference-time reranking and search guidance—since a fixed PRM under best-of-n suffers only mild optimization pressure at practical $n$; use ORMs (or verifier labels) to cheaply filter distillation data. The limiting case that clarifies the whole space: with a perfect step-level oracle, PRM-guided RL dominates because credit assignment is exact—every observed pathology is a symptom of the PRM being a lossy, gameable approximation to that oracle.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** PRMs add a full model to train, version, and serve, and their scores drift stale as the policy's step style shifts, forcing periodic relabeling on fresh rollouts; auto-labeling by Monte Carlo rollouts is compute-heavy (dozens of completions per step). Rule verifiers push cost into engineering—sandboxes, test-suite curation, equivalence checkers—and into audit rotas for coverage holes. Most production reasoning stacks in 2025 land on the hybrid: rules for RL reward, a learned scorer for best-of-n at serving time, with the divergence monitor above as a standing dashboard.
* **Red Flag:** Proposing a learned PRM as the direct RL reward "for better credit assignment" without any independent verifier in the loop, or reading a rising PRM score during training as evidence the model's reasoning is improving.
