### Q: Execute continued pretraining: data-mixture replay, learning-rate re-warming, tokenizer extension, and stopping criteria.
* **Difficulty:** Senior
* **Category:** Training
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** Continued pretraining adapts a base model to a new domain or language with the plain next-token objective. The working recipe: mix a replay fraction of original-distribution data against forgetting, re-warm the learning rate to a reduced peak and re-decay it over the new budget, extend the tokenizer with mean-initialized embeddings only when domain fertility is poor, and stop on the two-curve tradeoff between domain gain and general regression, not on a token count.
* **The Deep Dive:** The training objective is a mixture, $\mathcal L(\theta)=(1-\alpha)\,\mathbb E_{x\sim D_{\mathrm{new}}}[\ell(x;\theta)]+\alpha\,\mathbb E_{x\sim D_{\mathrm{replay}}}[\ell(x;\theta)]$, where the replay stream approximates the original pretraining distribution. Replay is the primary anti-forgetting lever: for mild distribution shifts even a fraction around $\alpha\approx 0.01$ measurably flattens the regression on general-domain loss, while strong shifts such as a new natural language typically need $\alpha$ in the 0.1 to 0.25 range. You rarely have the true pretraining mix, so an open web-scale proxy corpus stands in; it is imperfect but the ablation is unambiguous — set $\alpha=0$ on a distant domain and watch general perplexity climb monotonically while domain loss falls. Domain-internal mixture weights (subdomains, code versus prose, quality tiers) are tuned on small proxy models first, because mixture effects rank-order across scale far more reliably than absolute losses do.

  The learning-rate schedule is the second lever, and its failure modes are symmetric. Continuing at the tiny final LR of the original cosine underfits the new data; re-warming to the original peak maximizes adaptation but causes a transient loss spike on both distributions and is itself a major driver of forgetting. The standard compromise is to re-warm linearly to a reduced peak — often a fraction of the original, lower for closer domains — then cosine re-decay across the planned budget $T$:

  $$
  \eta(t)=\eta_{\min}+\tfrac{1}{2}\left(\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}-\eta_{\min}\right)\left(1+\cos\!\left(\pi t/T\right)\right).
  $$

  Because this couples the schedule to a budget you must fix in advance, open-ended programs increasingly use warmup-stable-decay: hold a constant plateau LR and branch short cooldown decays whenever a release checkpoint is needed, so continued pretraining can resume from the plateau without re-warming damage.

  Tokenizer extension is justified by fertility, the average tokens per word (or per byte) on domain text. A Latin-centric vocabulary can tokenize a new script at two to three times the fertility of a purpose-built one, wasting context and compute quadratically in attention. The procedure: train a domain tokenizer, merge its novel tokens into the base vocabulary, and initialize each new embedding as the mean of its old-tokenizer decomposition, $e_{\mathrm{new}}=\tfrac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k}e_{z_i}$, applying the same construction to the LM-head rows (or once, if tied). This is falsifiable by ablation: mean initialization starts training essentially at the old model's loss because each new token initially behaves like the average of its pieces, whereas random initialization produces a large initial spike and slower convergence. A worked example: extending a 32k Llama-style vocabulary with roughly 20k Chinese tokens cuts Chinese fertility approximately in half, halving sequence lengths — doubling effective context and cutting serving cost on domain text before any quality gain is counted. The limiting cases bound the design space: $\alpha\to 1$ is inert continuation with no adaptation; $\alpha\to 0$ with a full re-warm on a distant domain is maximal forgetting; extending the vocabulary for an English technical domain whose fertility is already near 1.3 buys almost nothing while invalidating every embedding-dependent artifact. Stopping is eval-based: track domain validation loss and a general-capability suite, and select the checkpoint where domain improvement per additional tokens falls below threshold or general regression exceeds an agreed budget.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** Replay data carries licensing and governance obligations distinct from the domain corpus, and proxy-replay quality quietly bounds how much forgetting you can buy back. Tokenizer extension breaks artifact compatibility — quantized checkpoints, speculative-decoding draft models, and anything assuming fixed embedding shapes must be rebuilt, and serialized token-id sequences become invalid. Continued pretraining erodes chat behavior, so budget a post-CPT SFT and alignment pass and evaluate the final aligned model, not the raw checkpoint. Cost is dominated by tokens, which is why mixture and LR sweeps happen on proxy models first.
* **Red Flag:** Continuing at the original run's final decayed learning rate "to be safe" (underfitting the domain), or re-warming aggressively with zero replay (maximal forgetting) — and randomly initializing new token embeddings, which forfeits the entire point of starting from a pretrained model for those rows.
