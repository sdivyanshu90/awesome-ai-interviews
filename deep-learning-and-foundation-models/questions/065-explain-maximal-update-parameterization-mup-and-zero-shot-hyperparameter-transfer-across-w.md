### Q: Explain maximal-update parameterization (muP) and zero-shot hyperparameter transfer across model width.
* **Difficulty:** Principal
* **Category:** Training
* **The 10-Second Pitch:** muP chooses width-dependent multipliers, initialization variances, and learning rates (an abc-parameterization) so that every layer's activations and every layer's per-step updates stay $\Theta(1)$ in coordinate size as width grows — making the optimal learning rate and related hyperparameters approximately width-invariant, so you tune a small proxy and transfer the settings zero-shot to the large model.
* **The Deep Dive:** An abc-parameterization writes each weight as $W = n^{-a} w$, initializes $w \sim \mathcal{N}(0, n^{-2b})$, and trains with learning rate $\eta\, n^{-c}$, where $n$ is width; a parameterization is a choice of exponents per layer type. Standard parameterization (SP) uses fan-in init with one global LR, and it is not width-consistent under training: consider the readout $z = W_{\mathrm{out}} h$ with $h \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$. After one Adam-like step, the update $\Delta W_{\mathrm{out}}$ has coordinates of size $\eta$ and correlates with $h$, so the logit change is a sum of $n$ correlated terms, $\Delta z \approx \Delta W_{\mathrm{out}} \cdot h = \Theta(\eta\, n)$. Keeping logit updates $\Theta(1)$ therefore forces $\eta \propto 1/n$ on some layers — but other layers (embeddings) want $\eta = \Theta(1)$, so a single global LR cannot be right at all widths, and the empirically optimal LR drifts as you widen the model. That is precisely why small-model sweeps mis-tune large models under SP. muP fixes the exponents so each layer individually satisfies two desiderata as $n \to \infty$: activations stay $\Theta(1)$ per coordinate, and updates change activations by $\Theta(1)$ (features actually learn — "maximal update" without blowup). For Adam the operative rules are:

  ```text
  layer type            init variance     LR scaling        multiplier
  embedding / input     O(1)              constant          1
  hidden n x n          1 / fan_in        eta / n           1
  readout (unembed)     1 / fan_in        eta / n           1 / n
  attention logits      use 1/d_h instead of 1/sqrt(d_h)
  ```

  Worked example: tune a width-256 proxy and find optimal hidden LR $3 \times 10^{-3}$. Scaling to width 8192 (ratio 32), the hidden-layer LR becomes $3 \times 10^{-3} / 32 \approx 9.4 \times 10^{-5}$, the readout multiplier shrinks by 32, embedding LR stays put, and attention logits are divided by $d_h$ rather than $\sqrt{d_h}$ — no re-tuning sweep at the large width. The headline validation (Tensor Programs V): hyperparameters tuned on a 40M-parameter proxy transferred to GPT-3 6.7B outperformed the original published 6.7B run, with tuning cost around 7 percent of the pretraining budget.

  The falsifiable verification is the coordinate check: instantiate the model at several widths (e.g., 128 to 4096), train a handful of steps on identical data, and plot the mean absolute coordinate size of each layer's activations and activation-updates against width on log axes. Under muP every curve is flat; under SP some curves grow or decay as power laws in width — and any implementation bug (a missed multiplier, a fused layer that bypasses the rule) shows up as a sloped line within minutes of compute, before any expensive run. The limiting cases mark the boundary of the guarantee: transfer holds across width, and empirically usefully across depth and batch size only with additional care (depth-muP and related work address depth explicitly); it does not transfer hyperparameters whose optimum is tied to dataset scale, like regularization strength or total training duration.
* **Production Reality & Tradeoffs:** muP's operational payoff is methodological: scaling-law sweeps become cleaner because every point on the curve is near-optimally tuned rather than tuned-at-one-scale, and it removed a major source of frontier-run risk — several public efforts (Cerebras-GPT, MiniCPM, and others) tuned proxies and transferred. Costs: every layer's multiplier and LR group must be threaded through the training stack (optimizer param groups, checkpoint resume, fused kernels), coord checks must gate every refactor, and proxy conclusions can still mislead when the large run changes data mixture or sequence length alongside width. Base-width conventions differ across implementations, so imported muP configs need re-verification.
* **Red Flag:** Claiming muP means "just scale the LR by one global factor" — the rules are per-layer-type, and the attention-logit change from $1/\sqrt{d_h}$ to $1/d_h$ matters; or asserting transfer across depth, data scale, or tokenizer changes when the guarantee is about width, verified only by a flat coord check.
